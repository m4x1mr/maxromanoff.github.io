Delivering Value to Our Delivery Customers


The store of launching the PC Express Pass grocery subscription service for Loblaws.

Timeline: Oct 2022 - Mar 2023

Role: Product Designer

Team: Myself, Allan D. (Design Lead)

PC Express is Loblaws’ answer to a customer need for receiving groceries by ordering online. By servicing the largest grocery chain in Canada — Loblaws and its subsidiaries — PC Express has accumulated hundreds of thousands of repeat customers and billions in sales. Ever since its inception, however, fees have been slowly increasing on the platform to help offset the rise in operating costs.


The problem was trivial: due to our high fee structure, customers were churning from our platform to either shop in-store or shop at a competitor. And thus our challenge was: How might we reduce fees for our customers, yet continue to build revenue to support the business?


Introducing PC Express Pass: a subscription service for customers to receive $0 fees on regular grocery pickup and delivery, and receive reduced fees on additional services such as Rapid Delivery (with DoorDash), and immediacy deliveries (within 3 hours).


PC Express Pass went live in March 2023 and became available on both web and app, across almost all of our banners. I worked on the program end-to-end; kicking off with a design sprint, then conducting research into competitors, designing out the experience across web and app, and working closely with development, business and marketing teams to get this out the door.

When we started this initiative, one of the success metrics I defined during our design sprint was: the number of people that’d stay subscribed, following the end of their free-trial. As of writing, we’re nearing 1 year of PC Express Pass being live and I think it’s safe to say we blew that out of the water.

+1.46M orders placed with Pass

Over $200M in sales

Over $9M saved in fees

Though this became one of Loblaw Digital’s biggest initiatives of 2023, and one of their most successful launches of all time, getting here and achieving these results wasn't easy.

Kicking off the design sprint

As an outcome of the design sprint, I worked with my design lead on defining the three parts of the journey for PC Express Pass. We looked at what competitors, such as UberOne and Instacart+, did in their experiences and narrowed it down to 3 core aspects: discovery, the core sign up experience, and managing the subscription. We white boarded this and tried to define what the experience would look like at all 3 stages, as well as what communications (i.e. emails) would be part of each stage.

For each part of the stage, we had a tenet that we followed to hold us accountable and ensure we were solving the right problems. Starting with discovery:


1. Ensuring that awareness was widespread of the program.
Customers needed to know throughout the journey, and more specifically in cart and checkout, that they have a way around the fees.

2. Signing up was quick and easy.
If customers are about to purchase their groceries, we should have a frictionless experience that reduces the deviation from that flow.

3. Managing the subscription had to be simple.
Have you ever tried unsubscribing from LinkedIn Premium? An easy way to cancel was something we prioritized, which we later iterated on and included a survey to help our research team gain rationale for cancellations.

The app cancellation flow, before the survey was included to get customers' rationale for cancellation.

Our first challenge: branding

Though it felt like we had 90% of the work done after the sprint, we came to realize that our branding and colours needed work. We saw in usability testing that customers did not notice our ad units sprinkled throughout the experience (banner blindness); an assumption we had was the yellow colour we were using was off-brand and didn’t capture enough attention. Working closely with our branding and design teams, we aligned on a deep red to accent our existing brighter red that we use so much throughout PC Express today. A learning from this challenge was to start with more recognizable colours right off the bat, rather than going wide.

Once that was decided on, I worked on figuring out "how would this colour affect the experience across the journey?", and "how could we make this memorable for customers as they continue to use PC Express?" I used this excessively when it came to ad units, especially on the modal that would appear on-entry for non-subscribed customers. The goal across the board was to make sure customers were not signing up because they didn't want to, rather than from lack of awareness.

Our next challenge: technical constraints

So remember the second tenet I mentioned? Signing up had to be quick and easy. And we thought we did that in the experience. The happy path flow was you’d open the sign up modal, select your plan, click sign up and you’re in. For what that looks like in a gif, look below.

The initial sign up flow that we had tested with customers after the design sprint. (Desktop web experience shown)

Well this flow started to be questioned when we realized we couldn’t charge credit cards on a recurring basis. As PC Express never had it’s own subscription program in the past, achieving this would be a major lift, so we used a third-party vendor to help us. The customer impact? Well this meant that customers would have to enter their credit card information for the program to be stored in the third-party’s backend system, even if they used PC Express and already had a credit card saved. This changed the flow that we’d designed by adding extra steps for customers to complete.

The resultant happy path flow after our technical constraints were considered. (Desktop web experience shown)

Honestly I was very upset with this approach. This went against our second tenet of needing the experience to be as simple as possible. Now, even though I’m a frequent shopper of PC Express, I was also the designer, so I had to step back. What I found in usability testing is that no customers actually mentioned this as being a negative part of the experience — even when being asked about those form fields in the flow. What the business team concluded was customers will likely still be motivated enough to remove fees from the experience, even if it means to spend ~30 seconds to enter in their credit card details again. A learning from this challenge was, though a designed experience could be the best possible experience, it doesn’t necessarily mean an alternative is the opposite.

The final boss: a ton of coordination

As I mentioned, it all kicked off with a design sprint. Representatives from marketing, UX, business, and development were all involved in the kick-off for the project, and I was the primary product designer on the initiative until it’s launch, with assistance from my lead, Allan. Though this seemed like a lot, this project then blew up to include heavy involvement from legal, PC Financial, our third-party vendor, and more effort from marketing than we initially imagined.


Now, how was this a challenge? Well, how was it not? Finding calendar times, gaining alignment from so many people on the smallest things, coordinating communications between legal teams, marketing and UX teams, and much, much more.


In retrospect, I don’t think there really is a way to solve for situations like this. Though Loblaw Digital operates like a startup, it still is under the Loblaw parent company which operates like not-a-startup… Large launches will require large engagements.

Learnings and takeaways

I had to do a lot and wear a lot of hats on this project. This might be the least UX-artifact-heavy project I’ve worked on, but somehow it felt like the most? I worked a ton with our marketing teams because content was so important on a project like this. We always had to make sure what they were sharing in ads and media matched what we were sharing in the experience to our customers.


Closely partnering with development was a must as well. We always had impromptu calls and deep walkthroughs of flows and rules for things in the experience. Throwing in the third-party layer as well, it brought us to empathize with each other as it was a service we were all new to and had to learn.


That stuff being said, I think my biggest learning was just working with a lot of different teams and getting to know a lot of people. I made great friends on our marketing side after being down and dirty in this project and I’m very thankful for it.
