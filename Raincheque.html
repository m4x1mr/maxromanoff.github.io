Job-Loss Insurance for the Mass Underserved

UX Internship
Role: Designer
Timeline: May - Aug 2019



Genoa Labs is an innovation team within RBC Insurance tasked in researching the mass underserved market (the low-middle income customer segment that is seemingly uninterested in purchasing traditional insurance). Prior to me joining the team, a lot of research was done to discover that millennials weren't purchasing insurance products as much as previous generations. Co-creation sessions were held, which led to the team identifying job-loss as a potential winning product. When I joined, our challenge was then set up to be: how do we position this product to better address our market’s needs?

Throughout my 4-months on the team, my role was to ideate and design the first prototypes for the website. I also worked closely with the product manager on the product & MVP strategy, content design and the visuals. In the end, I also made a montage to advertise our team internally within RBC!

The Process

Surveying Market Segments

When I joined the team, our team wanted to gain better insight into what peoples’ thoughts were around job-loss; therefore, to get quick responses, I launched surveys on UserTesting to understand their feelings. I’m lucky to have never lost a job before, so I had to ask individuals about things like the hardest things about the entire experience of losing their job(s) as well as about any advice they would give to someone if they were in the same boat.

“The hardest part [of losing a job] was dealing with the stress, financials and pride.“

“Try to save as much money as you can and apply to everything and anything you can find. Take care of yourself and remember you aren’t alone…”



When collecting and analyzing the data in Excel, I noticed emergent themes surrounding the financial hardship (this was expected), as well as the super-prominent emotional hardship (less expected). I found that the respondents spoke more about support and keeping themselves busy than they did about the actual financials. This was interesting data to consider, especially since we were heading towards a more financially-focused product.

Journey Mapping

Through understanding our segments' feelings, our team had a better idea of how to create a comprehensive initial journey map. First impressions are everything, so we wanted to look at the ideal-state purchase journey. We now knew that users could be in a fragile state when discussing job-loss (in addition to their financial motivations), therefore we saw the eligibility criteria (in stage 4) as a solid area of opportunity for extra clear content.

I found in my research that users could feel "hopeless" and irritated, therefore we wanted to make sure exclusions were clearly explained with reasoning behind them. Though we had our vision of long into the future, this journey map was created to help us visualize our MVP launch.

Competitive Analysis

Parallel to the consumer research, I looked into the existing state of job-loss, with things such as eligibility requirements for employment insurance, insight into records of employment (ROEs), and provincial laws behind severance pays. This research fed into our content strategy, exclusion criteria and our strategy for a second MVP that could be launched. I learned that ROEs can provide confirmation about an individual's employment type at a company, as well as find out how or why they were laid off.

In addition to that research, we looked at existing companies such as Lemonade to help inform some of our design's direction. Although it wasn't the only insurance company we looked at, we liked some primary features such as its transparent policy (put on Github), how clear their exclusions and sign-up process were, and its modern design (different from traditional insurance).

Early Sketching

Getting into the design, the Product Manager and I worked on the question flow that would be used to determine someone's eligibility; some of these questions would address our fears of anti-selection (i.e: only appealing only to those we were intending on excluding).

We initially asked questions such as: Company Name, Company Size, Full-Time Employee Status, Knowledge of Being Laid Off, Income, etc, but soon realized that a lot of these answers could be obtained on an ROE during claims. Our primary goal was to address the problem of trust and try to reject certain applicants right away, rather than take their money and reject them later during time of claims. The problem we faced was that some of these questions were not available on an ROE, therefore we wouldn't be able to verify them (either initially or during time of claim). For the time being, we ideated a form that could potentially be downloaded and given to an employer for verification.

Design Concepts & Testing

After the sketching phase, I created prototypes in Sketch and iterated multiple times following feedback on UserTesting. We heard feedback on all parts of the design, from the content, to the product itself, to the way we designed certain elements on the page.

— Ambiguous Questions

As we predicted, this ended up causing a lot of questions from our participants. Some of our questions such as “Which province do you live in?”, “What’s your email?” were asked to generate leads, however, participants were questioning why this was needed as some of the information could be gathered from the ROE.

A result of this problem, was that we began to add small descriptions under the heading/question to explain why we were asking each question.

— Plans by Income Group

We initially offered 3 tiers of plans by income group, as our research showed that layoffs were correlated to income groups. However, despite offering a catered option, lower income users really disliked this. We found that they felt excluded and that it wasn’t personalized, and wanted the option for the higher income plan even if they could not afford it.

For the final design, we opted for a calculator that would provide a more personalized option for each type of income. Each income, however, would receive their own 3 tiers of options, if they did in fact want to go for the most expensive coverage option.

— Donut Charts

The Product Manager ideated Donut Charts as a way to help emulate how much our top-up could help, when coupled with EI. I designed it with our colours to be the darker purple. From our testing, we found that users liked the idea of the visuals, but did not understand it (poor execution on my part). I found that some missed the detail about the amount of weeks that they would be covered for, so that had to be made more salient. This was an important detail as this was the primary differentiator between each tiered plan.

In the end, we decided to completely scrap this visual and stick to the basics. We chose to do a slider that would show each plan change dynamically per income chosen.

Final Designs

Following a few testing sessions through UserTesting, I worked on refining some of the above issues into a more comprehensive prototype. I found that the Product Manager and I were being held back a bit by focusing on the content, but at the same time, that was our primary objective to focus on. As we had a very simple site, with a linear sign-up, we had a lot more wiggle room to focus on how we worded every specific detail and a lot more time to think about the product strategy.

Results from this Prototype

**With specific quant data removed**

After over 5 launches on UserTesting.com (and a crazy amount of iterations), we ended up realizing that it's still an insurance product (surprise!).

We found that despite our efforts, the problem of anti-selection with early adopters & participants (mostly high-risk users were signing up) was still very prevalent. In addition, despite our efforts at transparency, we found the opposite reaction with our participants as they hated seeing the amount of exclusions. We knew we couldn't have a perfect product, therefore there'd have to be some sort of middle-ground.

On a positive note, we found that almost all participants found the design and idea trustworthy; some have attributed that to the RBC branding in the footer. All participants found the process simple and easy to follow. And most participants liked the design, whitespace and illustrations, and found it refreshing compared to what they're usually used to seeing.

What I Learned from this Internship

I was exposed to consumer research, design research, UI design, marketing, business, and video editing all while working in a startup environment for 4 months. This was the first time I've been exposed to Lean Startup within the workplace and working so quickly and iteratively. It was also exciting working with so many people higher up in the organization and seeing how many different teams this type of project brings together.

Before working on this team, this is something I would’ve never expected from a large company. Being able to basically “wing-it” when it came to our methods of problem solving compared to having a systemic way of going about a process is refreshing. I also loved the fact that everyday was spent working on something different. One day I could be doing only UI, and the next, I could be working on strategy and help identify issues that could seriously affect the final product. It definitely wasn't dull, and gets me excited to work in something mixed in strategy and design.
