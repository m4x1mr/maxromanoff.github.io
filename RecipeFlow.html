An SMS Chatbot for Recipes

Hackathon Project
Role: Product Manager
Timeline: May 2019 (2 Days)
Team: Max (me), David Paul, Kartik Budhiraja



This project was part of a 36-hour hackathon hosted by Ryerson University (RU Hacks). Working in a team of 3 — me being the Product Manager and my friends being the developers — we ended up winning a $1000 prize through a side-challenge sponsored by iTradeNetwork!

The theme for the overall hackathon was Around the World, or creating a project involving Communications and Global Data Exchange and any technology that can help with issues that plague societies. We found this to be very generic, so we looked for a bit more guidance at some of the side-challenges. As a result, the team and I opted for iTradeNetwork’s challenge of making something cool to improve or innovate the food and beverage industry as we felt that there was lots of opportunity to try and solve for.

Right away, we wanted to explore food-wastage, and take a look at that at a global scale. Although we knew we wouldn't be able to just get rid of this problem completely, we tried to find a niche in which we could comfortably take a seat in. That niche was dealing with leftover ingredients after a bad-case of overbuying.

The Process

The Problem with Food Waste

Getting even more niche, we started to focus on the Canadian market. With my research, I discovered that Canadians, specifically, throw out about 63% of their edible food, amounting to about 2.2M tonnes annually (or about 140kg per household). This to me was ridiculous, because even if this was reduced to just 50%, each household could reduce their food waste by almost 30kg. Addressing food-wastage at this scale could have serious impacts on the environment, so we tried to understand why.

Why Was This Happening?

Although proper planning, storage and preparation are obvious answers, a less obvious one is the lack of “thriftiness” in the kitchen. One thing that the USA's Environmental Protection Agency recommends is planning a leftovers night to ensure that nothing in the fridge ends up needlessly in the trash, as well as better education about labels such as "best-before" vs. "expiry".

Despite this, many people may still not know how to prepare an edible meal with the seemingly random ingredients leftover in their fridge. Our challenge was to figure out, how might we help users figure out what to cook with their leftover ingredients (in a cool and innovative way)?

Who Is This Affecting?

Being on a time-constraint, and being millennials ourselves, I decided to primarily focus on this user group.

With my research, I found that 59% of millennials take advantage of their phones when cooking in the kitchen; yet despite this, 31% of these millennials still believe that choosing what to cook is the least enjoyable part. Comparing this with our own experiences, we too found bottlenecks in using technology due to the shear amount of choices involved (resonating with the latter statistic), however, cookbooks would almost always be our last choice to look at.

As a result we developed this Jonathan persona, to represent someone that dislikes traditional methods of searching for recipes, yet despite their access to technology, often feels overwhelmed by the amount of options there are online. This is someone who would find themselves stuck on what to cook, yet have a fridge full of seemingly unrelated ingredients.

The Actual Hackathon

The First 24 Hours

With our problem of thriftiness in the kitchen, our persona who loves using his phone in the kitchen, and our challenge of making something "cool" we ideated an SMS chatbot which would help by sending recipes based on leftover ingredients sent by the users. Before moving any further with this idea, we wanted to work backwards and see if this was doable; we came up with using Twilio to help us send the texts, and then use Spoonacular API to help us gather the recipes.

I understood from our research that planning is a huge aspect to mitigate food waste, therefore our main value proposition focused on that by allowing users to search for recipes by combinations of ingredients, rather than the other way around. Cookbooks require users to search by recipe(s) and find ingredients after-the-fact; this lacks convenience and isn’t always accessible. Google fares better with widespread accessibility, however it can overwhelm users with the vast amount of choices, faring worse for our target users.

I worked on the main process flows for our system (being the initial sign-up and the actual interaction for receiving a recipe) to help the developers anticipate the data to be sent back and forth between the user and the system. A sign-up flow would be important in confirming the users' sign-up, and the recipe request flow was important to determine edge-cases where the system could break by certain inputs by the user.

The rest of the night ended up being slow as I then spent the night testing bugs with the developers.

The Last 12 Hours

During the last 12-hour set, I slowly started working on our pitch deck before moving onto the design and branding of our site.

We went with name of RecipeFlow and a logo of a "fast plate" to get our point across that that's what we're in business for. I ideated that our site would be a single page with a single text-field to enter your phone number, anything extra would be unnecessary.

Our Submission

After 36-hours, we pitched our product to a bunch of judges and other students. As mentioned, we delivered a website and a chatbot through SMS that sends 3 recipes based on ingredients sent in by the user. Click here to view the website and sign up!

Different From Other Projects

Although it was my first hackathon, I'm really proud of myself and my team that we created something so innovative and cool within 36-hours and that our efforts were recognized with a category win. The teamwork aspect of projects like this are unmatched and the adrenaline of having something launched this quickly is definitely a plus. In addition, making my first pitch deck and learning more about conversational design was also a great outcome of this.

Looking back as I'm writing this (May 2020), I'm definitely considering revisiting this project and pushing it forward more. I feel that something like this could have been a great addition to many quarantined families looking to get active in the kitchen, or even just curious teens looking to make something quick from something in their fridges.
